# Win the Old Game While You Build Your Exit: AI Interview Practice as a Trainable Loop Instead of a Choice Between Job Hunt and Reinvention

> "Testing is a powerful means of improving learning, not just assessing it." — Henry L. Roediger III and Jeffrey D. Karpicke, *Test-Enhanced Learning* (2006)

Adrian is back in the room. Not the same building — a different company, a different floor — but the same room in every way that used to matter: a table, a laptop, an engineering manager named Dana with the particular tiredness of someone carrying three open positions, and the question he has fumbled in every interview since the layoff. "So," she says, glancing at the gap on the resume, "walk me through what you've been doing since your last role." Eight months ago that question was a trapdoor. His voice would drop, the apology would arrive before the answer, and he would watch the interviewer's attention politely close like a laptop lid. He knows exactly how it used to go, because for the past three weeks he has been reading transcripts of himself doing it.

That is the part nobody in the room can see. Since his first contract shipped, Adrian has spent eleven evenings — twenty minutes each, after the second bedtime story, the same shift the applications used to get — rehearsing this exact question out loud against an AI interviewer. Not reading about interviews. Answering, aloud, into a counterpart that asked the skeptical follow-up, kept the transcript, and showed him the same weakness until he could see it himself: he apologized for the gap before describing it, every single time. Rep by rep he watched the apology shrink in the transcript and then vanish. So when Dana asks the question now, he pauses, comfortably, and answers: "I've been consulting. One client so far — I built them a support-docs assistant. It's live; I can show you." He turns the laptop. The URL loads. Same room. Same questions. Different man.

Here is the question this chapter exists to answer: if an interview is a live performance under pressure, why did you prepare for every one of yours by silently reading?

## The false belief: "I have to choose: job hunt OR reinvent myself"

Say it out loud, because it is running your calendar right now: *either I grind the job hunt — LeetCode, applications, interview prep — or I go all-in on the new game and build. Doing both means doing both badly.* The belief feels like discipline, and underneath the discipline there is a stickier version about allegiance: interviewing feels like betraying the new game, and building feels like betraying the family math that needs a paycheck. So most engineers end up half-running both — applying without preparing, building without shipping — and call the exhaustion a strategy.

This book's position, stated plainly: take the bridge. A paycheck is not a defeat and an interview is not beneath you — the salary that keeps the savings clock quiet is the cheapest way to buy the calm the new game is built with. The belief's real error is not moral; it is technical. It assumes the two games train separately — that an hour on interviews is an hour stolen from the build. The opposite is true: the interview is a trainable performance, the training loop is twenty minutes an evening, and the machinery you use to train it is the same AI counterpart you have been working beside since chapter 2.

## The concept: an interview is a performance — and performances are rehearsed

What an interview measures is not what the calendar invite says. It measures your ability to retrieve what you know and say it clearly *while a stranger's judgment is pressing on you* — retrieval under pressure, plus composure. Both are real skills; neither is engineering. No actor prepares for opening night by reading the play silently; no pilot meets an engine failure for the first time outside a simulator. Engineers are the exception: we prepare for the one high-stakes verbal performance in our professional lives by reading, and the first take of a performance — the worst take you will ever give — happens live.

The research is specific about why reps beat reading. Roediger and Karpicke found that re-reading wins five minutes later but loses a week later — repeatedly *tested* learners retained roughly 21 percentage points more, while the re-readers were more confident and remembered less. Smith, Floerke, and Thomas then put learners under acute stress — an impromptu speech and math in front of judges, which is to say an interview administered as science — and found stress gutted the restudy group's recall while retrieval-practiced material held. Composure is not a personality trait. It is a property of how the answer was stored. (Honesty note: the famous deliberate-practice claim has been trimmed by replication — targeted, feedback-rich reps improve the practiced thing; they do not make anyone elite.)

## The framework: The Rehearsal Loop

Deliberate practice applied to the interview: aloud, under questioning, with a transcript. Four steps.

1. **Pick the question you fumble.** One question, not a bank of two hundred. Deliberate practice attacks a named weakness; generic practice is just experience, and experience without feedback has already failed you. You know the question — it is the one your stomach answers before you do.
2. **Rep it aloud against an AI counterpart.** Aloud is load-bearing — retrieval, not recognition, builds the memory that survives pressure. Tell any chat model to interview you: ask the question, press two skeptical follow-ups, no compliments.
3. **Read the transcript and tag the weakness.** The transcript is the feedback forty real interviews never gave you. Tag one concrete, behavioral weakness per rep — "apologized for the gap before describing it," "result arrived in minute three" — quoted from your own words.
4. **Re-rep until the tag disappears.** Run the question again, hunting only that tag. When it is gone two reps running, pick the next question. You walk into the real room already holding a verdict — measured, recent, and yours.
